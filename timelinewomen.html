Women Timeline

Timeline of Womens History and accomplishments in Japan

Nara Period

Of the eight empresses that ruled Japan only six reigned.

Heian Period

The matriarchal family system that dominated Japanese social structures in ancient times is still in place.

Female feudal lords and independent women artists and writers left a distinct "feminine" imprint on the culture.

Women started inheriting property under their own names and manage it by themselves.

Edo period

Prostitutes flirt with members of the Imperial court, are setting the fashion trends and are enjoying a social standing higher than that of noble ladies.

Daughters are often sold, a pregnancy that results in a daughter are called a dog stomach and twin daughters are regularly abandoned.

Meiji period

Many girls marry at age 16.

Motherhood is the central task of women, and the education of women is allowed.

1867

Women need passports to travel on the Kiso Road.

1871

Education code established that students should be educated without any distinction of class or sex.

1872

The Japanese government issues a decree which states: "Any remaining practices of female exclusion on shrine and temple lands shall be immediately abolished, and mountain climbing for the purpose of worship, etc., shall be permitted".

1898

Cruelty is added to the grounds for a woman to divorce.

The law also allows divorce through a mutual agreement of the husband and wife.

1908

Husbands are no longer permitted to murder wives for infidelity.

1910

Few universities accept women.

1919

Raicho Hiratsuka creates the New Woman Association named Shin Fujin Kyokai with the goal to achieve rights of protection and inclusion through identifying a female class.

1920

The fight for women's political inclusion is at the forefront of the suffrage movement.

1921

The parliament of Japan overruled Article 5 of the Police Security Act and grant women the right to attend political meetings.

1923

100 leaders from multiple organizations come together to form the Tokyo Federation of Women's Organizations.

1924

The Women's Suffrage League is founded.

1925

Parliament passes the universal manhood suffrage bill, permitting men to vote free from any economic qualifications, excluding women.

1930

Arranged marriages continue and "love matches" are thought to be scandalous, specifically for the husbands, who are thought to be effeminate and unmanly. Click here to see LGBT Timeline in Japan

1945

Women in Japan receive the right to vote.

1970

The Women's Liberation Front is considered a radical activist group .

1978

Only 26 percent of Japanese people believe that women could be happy without marrying.

1924

The Women's Suffrage League is founded.

1980

Parents tend to spend more on their sons for private educational institutions than on their daughters.

1982

A poll conducted by the Prime Minister’s Office, shows that 70 percent of the Japanese people agree that a woman’s place is in the home and little girls should be brought up to be behave like ladies.

1986

The Equal Employment Opportunity Law takes effect.

1991

Elementary textbooks for 1992-1996 academic years are found to have a majority of the main characters in novels and important figures in history are overwhelmingly male.

1996

The Japanese government prepares the Plan for Gender Equality 2000.

1998

The General Assembly of the Anglican Church in Japan starts to ordain women.

1999

Law is passed that banned discrimination in the work place.

Basic law is passed that promotes equal rights for women.

The contraceptive pill is legalized.

2000

A study done in Japan shows that two thirds of Japanese women said they been victims of violence or verbal abuse, a quarter of Japanese women said they had been victims of serous acts of violence and sixteen percent said they had been beaten or kicked.

2001

The Cabinet Office is created, and the Council for Gender Equality and the Gender Equality Bureau are placed within the Cabinet Office.

Anti-domestic violence legislation is passed.

2003

Former Prime Minister Yoshiro Mori stated that women who didn’t have children where diminishing their responsibilities and should be denied their pensions.

2005

42.5 percent of women ion Japan have at least some post-secondary education.

2006

Japan ranks 11th among 12 industrialized nations in the proportion of women among all national assembly members, at 9.4 percent.

2007

190 women, a record number, won seats in elections for prefectural assemblies.

Japanese Health, Labor and Welfare minister Hakuo Yanagisawa is widely condemned for calling women “birth-giving machines” in a speech on the declining birth rate.

2008

Two women are named to the new 17-member cabinet.

2012

98.1% of female students and 97.8% of male students are now able to reach senior high school.

A government survey showed that more than half of the Japanese public supports the idea that husbands should be the workers and providers while wives should stay home, raise children, and do housework.

2014

Women make up 42.7% of the labour force of Japan.

2015

Article 733 of Japan’s Civil Code that states that women cannot remarry 6 months after divorce is now reduced to 100 days, any child born after 200 days of remarriage is the legal child of the current husband, and any woman who is not pregnant during the divorce to remarry immediately after divorce.

2016

Yuriko Koike become the first female governor of Tokyo.

2018

Tokyo Medical University has been exposed to prioritizing male applicants to enter the medical school when female applicants had been scoring higher on their entrance exams.

Japanese bullfighting organizers lifted a ban on women entering the bullfighting ring.

2020

In Japan's parliament women hold slightly less than 10% of seats despite a government goal for 30% of elected officials to be women.

Yuriko Koike, the first female governor of Tokyo is re-elected.
